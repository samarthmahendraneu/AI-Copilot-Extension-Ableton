# Drum Programming Reference

These are genre conventions and production principles — use them as a starting point
and adapt freely to what the user's prompt asks for.

## The bar-repeating contract
- You define ONE base bar. The engine clones it across every bar.
- The base bar should contain the full kick + snare + hat backbone.
- variation_bars ADD hits on top (fills, crashes) — they typically don't replace the backbone.
- Putting every essential hit in base_pattern gives the most consistent groove.

## The backbone (build in this order)
1. **Kick** — anchor on beat 0 (downbeat). Add 1–3 more per bar depending on genre.
2. **Snare/Clap** — the backbeat. In standard 4/4: snare on beat 1.0 and 3.0
   (the "2 and 4" of the bar when counting 1-4).
3. **Hats** — closed hats fill the subdivision (8ths or 16ths). This sets the energy.
4. **Accents** — open hat, ride, ghost snares, shaker for human feel.

## Snare placement
- In a 4-beat bar (0.0–3.0): the conventional backbeat snare sits on beats 1.0 and 3.0.
- This is the most common and recognisable placement — but many genres move it.
- Fills and rolls typically go in variation_bars so they don't repeat every bar.

## Velocity for drums
- Kick: 100–120 (consistent, it's the anchor)
- Snare backbone: 95–115. Ghost snares: 25–50.
- Closed hat: alternating 70–90 / 50–70 for a "tick-TOCK" feel.
- Open hat / ride: 85–105 (they ring out, they're features).

## Fills
- Typically on the last bar of a 4 or 8 bar phrase (bar index 3 or 7).
- A fill adds snare/tom hits in the 2nd half of the bar (beats 3.0–3.75).

## Things that tend to kill the groove
- Same velocity on every hat (sounds robotic)
- Hats on every 16th with no velocity variation (fatiguing)
- Moving the snare off the backbeat without a specific musical reason
- Relying on variation_bars for the kick and snare backbone
